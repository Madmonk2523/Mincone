5 Things Every First-Time Homebuyer in New York Should Know

Buying your first home is one of life's most exciting milestones. It's also one of the most complex financial transactions you'll ever undertake. In New York, the real estate market has unique characteristics that can catch first-time buyers off guard. Here's what you need to know before making an offer.

1. You Need an Attorney (It's Not Optional)

Unlike some states where real estate agents handle most of the transaction, New York requires—or at least strongly encourages—attorney involvement. Your attorney will:

  • Review and negotiate the contract of sale
  • Conduct due diligence on the property
  • Examine the title and order title insurance
  • Review mortgage documents
  • Attend the closing and ensure all documents are properly executed

Having an experienced real estate attorney on your side can save you thousands of dollars and prevent costly mistakes.

2. The Attorney Review Period is Your Safety Net

In New York, once you sign a contract, there's typically a 3-day attorney review period. During this time, either party's attorney can cancel the contract or request modifications. This is your opportunity to:

  • Have the contract thoroughly reviewed
  • Negotiate better terms
  • Add contingencies that protect your interests
  • Back out if issues are discovered

Never waive this period or sign a contract without attorney review—no matter how competitive the market seems.

3. Understand Co-ops vs. Condos

New York has a unique mix of co-ops and condominiums, and they're very different:

Co-ops

  • You're buying shares in a corporation, not real property
  • Require board approval (can be intensive)
  • Often have stricter financial requirements
  • May restrict subletting and renovations

Condos

  • You own real property (your unit)
  • Easier approval process
  • More flexibility for subletting and financing
  • Generally more expensive

4. Budget for Closing Costs

In New York, closing costs for buyers typically range from 2-4% of the purchase price. These include:

  • Title insurance (lender's and owner's policies)
  • Attorney fees
  • Mansion tax (if applicable, for properties $1M+)
  • Mortgage recording tax
  • Property taxes and homeowner's insurance escrows
  • Bank fees and points

Don't forget to budget for these beyond your down payment.

5. Get Pre-Approved Before You Start Looking

In competitive markets like New York, sellers often won't take your offer seriously without mortgage pre-approval. Pre-approval:

  • Shows sellers you're a serious buyer
  • Helps you understand your budget
  • Speeds up the closing process
  • Identifies potential credit issues early
